Empathy


In the Oxford Dictionary app on my Mac I searched for "empathy" and was instantly provided the following description:



I believe most of us consider ourselves emphatic beings, and mostly think of sociopaths in the lack thereof. Pondering over the concept, I attempted to think of when I last had a feeling of being emphatic. It can easily be confused with similar notions such as friendliness, sympathy, happiness and more. While I will not set out on a journey to distinguish them or to form one truth, I suggest the thought for you to think about.


Searching for a memory of an empathic moment, one was hard to pick out. Was it, for example, when I saw someone run to catch the tram only to just miss it – and feeling that? Or observing a mother with a newborn on her chest, endlessly yawning at the local cafe – and feeling her? I guess, and hope, that we all experience empathy in all sort of ways, in small moments and larger. But I did also think to myself, that often the feeling occurs in moments or stories of e.g. joy or sadness – "enlarged" emotional moments – and maybe less so in monotonous, everyday life.



Object agency


Objects can take many shapes and go by many names. Thing. Artefact. Article. Item. Piece. They are all around us. Often we got too many, seldom too few.

Inherent in a object-based enquiry exists a conundrum of objects as symbol of mass-consumption and consumerism, while also posing the option for objects as being meaningful. While both can be true, this project adapts a more positive note of objects as important and meaningful in our understanding of the world.

The things with which we live are not only a medium through which to think the world, but also form an integral part of living and acting and doing in that world. From a consistently Marxist and materialist perspective, things are part and parcel of society, culture, politics and values, and constitute a key medium through which we can understand and analyse social identity, change and transformation. (Tilley 2011, p. 350)



Workshops


From an empirical vantage point, I intend to substantiate this research with quantitative inquiry using methodologies of social research and theoretical reflection. Preliminary, semi-structured interviews with expert practitioners will inform and help shape object-based inquires, and also provide insight into current design practices and tools of participatory design. Building on interview discoveries and examples, I will investigate objects as mediator of empathic one-on-one encounters through a series of workshops — akin to object-based social experiments of small-scale.



The emotional metaphor


In Bruno Latour’s From Realpolitik to Dingpolitik Or How to Make Things Public he states: “For too long, objects have been wrongly portrayed as matter-of-fact. This is unfair to them, unfair to science, unfair to objectivity, unfair to experience.” (p.158). Alfred Gell (1998) likewise argues that objects take effect not exclusively by communicating conventionally understood messages but suggests a ‘grey area where semiotics inference merges with hypothetical inferences of a non-semiotic kind’ (p.14). Gell phrases this notion abductions – ‘neither ‘semiotic conventions’ or ‘laws of nature’ but something in between’ (p. 15). As such, one may contend objects as commonly understood, neither as systems or semiotics but rather open up for the non-iconic discourse that a chair is more than a device with which to sit.


Lusebrink seems to similarly address Gell’s ‘grey area’ where objects exist as tools for meaning-making. Lusebrink speaks of visual symbols and metaphors’s ability to give meaning and form to language, feelings or energy in the subconscious; as a mediator between ‘the body and psyche’ (2012, p. 49), concurring with the notion that richness of emotion is often conceptualised and understood using metaphors and metonymies (Abbott & Forceville 2011; Feng & O’halloran 2012; Lusebrink 2012; Kövecses 1990), and to help build a system of thinking and concepts that can guide in confusion and aid in making ‘something unfamiliar familiar’ (Moeschberger & DeZalia 2014, p. 6).


Christopher Tilley likewise suggests the metaphor as central to both material culture and language as a means of linkage ‘between seemingly disparate social and material domains’ (2006, p. 62).

One may argue that visual objects can extend beyond their design intent and physicality, and give meaning and form to language, concurring with Tilley on the non-verbal materiality of objects, and how “material forms complement what can be communicated in language” (2006, p. 62).
